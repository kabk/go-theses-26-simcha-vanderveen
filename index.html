NEGOTIATING
THE GRID

Abstract

This research document explores how pre-defined visual systems—such as grids and templates—shape decision-making in drawing and redistribute authorship between the maker and the structure itself. It investigates how images are not only created through intention and expression, but also through organisational frameworks that guide, limit and filter visual choices.

What specifically stands out to me is the moment when a grid is introduced before drawing begins. A simple structure immediately changes how I move my hand, how I divide attention and how the image is allowed to appear. In this thesis, I focus on a working method in which the same image is repeatedly redrawn within different grid systems, in order to examine moments of dominance, resistance, adaptation and compromise between image and framework. I also include a collective workshop as part of my practice, using shared grids and prompts to observe how multiple makers negotiate the same system.

My interest in this topic grows from my own long-standing fascination with structured surfaces such as colouring pages, templates and squared paper, and from my personal experience of feeling both guided and restricted by visual frameworks in my drawing practice.

Introduction

My first fascination with drawing was not really about what exactly I was drawing, but about the surfaces I was drawing on. As a child, I was drawn to notebooks with margins, squared paper, colouring books and printed templates. I liked the feeling that something was already there before I started. The page was not empty, and I did not have to invent everything myself. I could enter a structure and respond to it. Only much later did I realise that this attraction to pre-defined formats had stayed with me and had gradually become central to the way I work.

Today, I notice that the moment I introduce a grid into my drawings, my behaviour as a maker changes. My hand slows down, my attention shifts from the whole image to small segments, and my decisions become more local and conditional. Instead of asking what I want the image to be, I start asking what is possible inside the structure that is already in front of me. The grid does not simply organise the page; it actively guides where I look, where I hesitate and where I continue.

This research grows directly out of that personal experience. I am interested in what happens when I deliberately give part of my control to a pre-defined visual system, and in how this changes my role as a maker. By repeatedly placing the same image into different grid structures, I explore how drawing becomes a process of negotiation rather than free composition. The image is continuously reshaped by the logic of the grid, while my own authorship shifts into both inventing form as well as responding to constraints.

Chapter 1

Entering a surface that is already organised

subtitle

When a grid is placed onto a blank page, drawing no longer begins from an empty field, but from an already organised situation. In my own practice, this simple act immediately alters how I behave as a maker. Where I start, where I pause, where I return and where I abandon a line are no longer decided only by intention or expression, but by the internal logic of the structure that has been introduced.

This shift in behaviour is the starting point of my research. I am interested in what happens when the grid is no longer treated as a neutral background tool, but acknowledged as an active participant in the drawing process. I do not use the grid to improve composition or accuracy. Instead, I use it to make visible how much of imagemaking is or can be shaped by pre-existing organisational frameworks.

Working in this way has gradually transformed how I understand authorship in my own practice. I no longer experience myself as the sole origin of the image. The drawing emerges through a relationship between my hand and a structure that is already in place before I begin.

This understanding resonates strongly with the ideas introduced in The Death of the Author. Although Barthes writes about literature, the redistribution of authorship he describes becomes tangible in my own drawing process. When working with a grid, authorship is no longer concentrated in a single, sovereign decision-making subject, but is shared with the system that structures the conditions of action. The grid pre-organizes space, rhythm, and proportion before any expressive choice takes place, so that each mark emerges in dialogue with an already established framework rather than from an autonomous artistic intention. In this sense, the drawing unfolds less as the execution of a pre-existing idea and more as a negotiation with constraints that actively shape what can appear. Barthes famously argues that meaning should no longer be traced back to a single origin, writing that “The birth of the reader must be at the cost of the death of the Author.” Read through the lens of my practice, this shift away from a privileged authorial voice can be understood as a shift toward the operative role of systems and structures—here, the grid itself—which participate in the production of the work. The grid functions not merely as a neutral support, but as an agent that redistributes creative responsibility, transforming authorship into a distributed process that arises from the interaction between rule, gesture, and perception rather than from an individual will alone.

This redistribution becomes especially apparent in the way the grid directs attention. It determines which areas receive care and which remain empty. It encourages continuation in certain directions and interrupts others. The grid operates as a quiet form of instruction embedded within the surface itself.

My practice therefore begins with a simple but deliberate choice: I place myself inside a system before I draw.

Chapter 2

The grid as infrastructure

subtitle

Thinking about drawing as a negotiation with structure also changes how I understand images more generally. Images today do not exist in isolation; they are shaped by formats, platforms and technical conditions that organise how they are produced, circulated and encountered. This broader perspective aligns with the way Hito Steyerl describes contemporary image culture in Duty Free Art. Steyerl shows that images are never neutral carriers of content, but are continuously formatted, compressed and governed by economic and institutional infrastructures that condition how they can appear and how they can be read.

Although my own drawings are slow and analogue, I recognise a similar logic operating at a somewhat smaller and more personal scale. In my practice, the grid functions as a local infrastructure. It establishes visibility, hierarchy and access before any image enters the page. In this sense, the grid is not simply a compositional aid, but a structuring system that produces its own order and its own limits for action.

This way of thinking is also reflected in the artistic practices that inform my own. In the work of Henri Jacobs, whom I interviewed, repetitive structures, patterns and grid-like systems often resemble educational or institutional formats. These visual languages suggest that structure is something inherited rather than invented, and that it already carries authority before it becomes an aesthetic choice. A recent conversation with Jacobs further strengthened my understanding of the grid as a cultural and organisational form that precedes individual authorship.

A similar attention to systems and procedures can be found in the practice of Sigrid Calon, whose modular and rule-based compositions generate visual outcomes through predefined operations rather than expressive gestures. What I take from her work is the idea that authorship can reside in the design of a system itself—in the formulation of rules and structures—rather than primarily in the execution of an image

My thinking about distributed authorship is further supported by the practice of Marijn van Kreij, whose work is centred on repetition, reworking and recomposing existing material. Through my earlier conversation with him, I became more aware of how authorship can shift away from production and towards processes of selection, organisation and framing. Although he does not work with grids, his approach resonates strongly with my interest in authorship as something that unfolds across systems and procedures rather than within a single expressive act.

Together, these perspectives allow me to position my own drawing practice within a broader contemporary understanding of systems, infrastructures and delegated agency. The grid in my work is therefore not a modernist formal device or a neutral tool. It operates as a local system that governs behaviour, visibility and decision-making, and actively participates in how images come into being.

Epilogue

This research document began with a simple and personal observation: that my way of drawing changes the moment a grid is introduced. Across the chapters, I have used this shift in behaviour as a method for thinking through authorship, control and structure in my own practice. What initially appeared to be a technical or organisational choice gradually revealed itself as a conceptual one. By placing a drawing inside a pre-defined visual system, I enter a situation in which attention, movement and decision-making are already partially scripted. Drawing no longer unfolds as the execution of an internal image, but as a continuous response to an external framework that actively shapes what I am able to see and do.

The question I posed at the beginning—who leads here, the image or the grid?—cannot be answered in a fixed or hierarchical way. Instead, this research shows that leadership is distributed and constantly negotiated. The image brings its own demands, references and material tensions, while the grid introduces a parallel logic that fragments, redirects and reorganises those demands. My role as a maker shifts accordingly. Rather than directing the work from a central position of control, I operate within an already structured field, adjusting, hesitating and responding to conditions that precede my gestures. Authorship, in this context, becomes less about origin and more about positioning: deciding which systems to enter, how strictly to follow them, and how long to remain inside their constraints.

My research document proposes drawing as an encounter with infrastructure. By treating the grid as a local system—one that governs visibility, hierarchy and access on the surface of the page—I make the normally invisible influence of structure materially present in the work itself. What emerges is not a rejection of authorship, but a reconfiguration of it. I locate authorship in the construction of situations, in the choice of formats and in the design of conditions under which images are allowed to transform. The drawings that result do not resolve the tension between image and structure; instead, they hold it open. In doing so, my practice affirms the grid not as a background device, but as a collaborator—one that continually reshapes my decisions, redistributes responsibility, and makes the act of drawing a shared process between hand, system and surface.
